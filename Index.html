01 — Overview

What is Data Communication?

📡

Transmission

The process of sending data from one device to another through a medium — wired or wireless — using encoded signals.

🔀

Protocols

Rules and standards (TCP/IP, HTTP, Ethernet) that govern how data is packaged, transmitted, and received across networks.

🔒

Security & Integrity

Techniques like error detection, encryption, and checksums that ensure data arrives accurately and securely.

🌐

Network Models

OSI and TCP/IP layered models that break communication into manageable, standardized layers from physical to application.

📶

Bandwidth & Capacity

Nyquist and Shannon theorems define the theoretical limits of data transmission speed over any channel.

🔁

Multiplexing

Techniques (TDM, FDM, WDM) that allow multiple signals to share a single channel simultaneously.

02 — Key Topics

What We Studied

01
Data vs. Signal Difference between analog and digital data, and how they are represented as signals.
02
Transmission Media Twisted-pair, coaxial cable, fiber optics, and wireless media (radio, microwave, infrared).
03
OSI & TCP/IP Models Seven-layer OSI model vs. four-layer TCP/IP model and their respective functions.
04
Encoding & Modulation NRZ, Manchester, AMI encoding; AM, FM, PM modulation for analog-to-digital conversion.
05
Error Detection & Correction Parity bits, CRC, Hamming codes — methods to detect and fix transmission errors.
06
Flow & Error Control Stop-and-wait, sliding window, Go-Back-N, and Selective Repeat ARQ protocols.
07
Switching Circuit switching vs. packet switching vs. message switching — trade-offs and applications.
08
IP Addressing & Routing IPv4, IPv6, subnetting, and how routers determine the best path for packets.
09
History of Data Comm From Morse's telegraph (1837) to fiber optics — the evolution of communication technology.
10
Wireless & Mobile Networks Wi-Fi standards, Bluetooth, cellular generations (2G → 5G), and spread-spectrum techniques.
03 — Historical Diagram

The Electric Telegraph

The telegraph (1837) was the world's first long-distance electrical communication system, encoding messages as pulses of current — the conceptual ancestor of all modern data networks.

Fig 1. Samuel Morse's Electric Telegraph System (ca. 1837–1844) — sender encodes message via key → current pulses travel wire → sounder at receiver decodes Morse code

04 — Inspiration

What Inspires Me

"The Internet is not just one thing; it's a collection of millions of ongoing human conversations."
— Howard Rheingold

What inspires me most about data communication is the invisible infrastructure that connects 5 billion people. A message I send from Accra reaches Tokyo in milliseconds — travelling through undersea cables, satellites, and routers — because thousands of engineers agreed on protocols.

  • The idea that open standards (TCP/IP) created more value than any single company could alone
  • How Africa's fibre-optic revolution is reshaping education and economic opportunity
  • The elegance of error-correcting codes — fixing mistakes without retransmitting
  • Seeing how today's 5G networks descended directly from Morse's 1837 telegraph
  • Dreaming of building network infrastructure that reaches rural communities in Ghana
